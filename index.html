Sentence Mechanics

Cut the clutter. Activate the passive. Make every word earn its place.

Passive Voice Strunk & White

Use active voice. Put the actor before the action. "The team fixed the bug" is stronger than "The bug was fixed by the team."

Active voice is direct, vigorous, and usually shorter. Passive obscures responsibility.

Before The report was reviewed by the manager.
After The manager reviewed the report.
Before Mistakes were made in the calculation.
After We made mistakes in the calculation.
Notice how active voice forces you to name the actor.
Nominalizations (Buried Verbs) Williams

Don't turn verbs into nouns. "Make a decision" → "decide." "Give consideration to" → "consider." Let verbs do the heavy lifting.

Nominalizations add syllables without adding meaning. They make prose feel bureaucratic and sluggish.

Before We performed an analysis of the data.
After We analyzed the data.
Before The implementation of the feature requires resources.
After Implementing the feature requires resources.
Expletive Constructions Williams

Avoid "There is/are" and "It is" sentence starters. These expletives delay the real subject and add empty words.

Starting with the real subject makes sentences more direct and often shorter.

Before There are three factors that affect performance.
After Three factors affect performance.
Before It is important that we finish by Friday.
After We must finish by Friday.
Wordy Phrases Zinsser

Replace wordy phrases with concise alternatives. "In order to" → "to." "Due to the fact that" → "because." "At this point in time" → "now."

Every unnecessary word is a small friction on the reader's attention. Multiply by hundreds and prose becomes exhausting.

Before In the event that the system fails, contact support.
After If the system fails, contact support.
Before For the purpose of testing, we created a mock server.
After For testing, we created a mock server.
Redundancy Strunk & White

Omit needless words. "Completely finished" → "finished." "Past history" → "history." "Future plans" → "plans." If a word adds nothing, cut it.

Redundancy suggests the writer doesn't trust their words—or their reader. Trust both.

Before The end result was a total success.
After The result was a success.
Before We need to collaborate together on this project.
After We need to collaborate on this project.

Writing Principles

Master the principles that make technical writing clear and effective.

Old Before New Williams

Start sentences with familiar information. New or complex information should come after the reader has context.

Readers need a foothold. Starting with known info creates a bridge to new concepts.

Before A distributed cache was implemented. Redis was chosen for this purpose.
After We implemented a distributed cache. For this cache, we chose Redis.
Stress Position Williams

Put the most important information at the end of a sentence. The final position carries natural emphasis.

Readers remember endings. Use this natural emphasis for your key point.

Before Performance improved by 40% after the optimization, which was significant.
After After the optimization, performance improved by 40%.
Subject-Verb Proximity Williams

Keep subject and verb close together. Long interruptions between them force readers to hold too much in working memory.

Readers parse subject-verb-object. Separating them creates cognitive strain.

Before The system, after being reviewed by the security team and updated with new patches, crashed.
After The system crashed after the security team reviewed it and applied new patches.
Positive Form Strunk & White

Say what something is, not what it isn't. "He was absent" beats "He was not present." Double negatives are especially confusing.

Negatives require mental translation. "Not unhappy" forces readers to compute "happy."

Before The feature is not unavailable to free users.
After The feature is available to free users.
Concrete Over Abstract Zinsser

Use specific, tangible terms. "Response time dropped from 2s to 200ms" beats "performance improved significantly."

Abstractions are vague. Concrete details build credibility and understanding.

Before The system handles a large volume of requests efficiently.
After The system handles 10,000 requests per second with 50ms latency.
Cut Qualifiers Zinsser

Remove hedging words: very, quite, rather, somewhat, fairly, really. They weaken your message.

Qualifiers signal uncertainty. If you're not sure, find out. If you are, commit.

Before This is a rather important and quite significant change.
After This is an important change.
Parallel Construction Strunk & White

Use matching grammatical structures in lists and comparisons. All items should follow the same pattern.

Parallelism creates rhythm and reduces cognitive load. Broken parallelism jars.

Before The tool supports parsing JSON, to validate XML, and CSV export.
After The tool supports JSON parsing, XML validation, and CSV export.
Characters as Subjects Williams

Make the actors in your story the grammatical subjects. "The team deployed" beats "Deployment was performed."

Readers follow characters. Abstract subjects ("Implementation," "Analysis") lack agency.

Before Investigation of the issue was undertaken by the SRE team.
After The SRE team investigated the issue.

Logical Sequencing

Order matters. Learn when to lead with conclusions vs. context.

Inverted Pyramid Journalism

Lead with the conclusion. Put your most important point first, then supporting details, then background. Busy readers can stop anytime and still get the key message.

Executives skim. Engineers interrupt. If your conclusion is on page 3, it may never be read.

Before We analyzed the logs, consulted with vendors, ran diagnostics... and concluded the database needs upgrading.
After The database needs upgrading. Our analysis of logs, vendor consultation, and diagnostics all point to this conclusion.
Context Before Detail Technical Writing

Give the big picture before diving into specifics. Readers need to know where they're going before following you there.

Details without context are noise. Context makes details meaningful.

Before Set timeout to 30s. Add retry logic. Update the config file. (Why are we doing this?)
After To improve reliability during network issues: set timeout to 30s, add retry logic, and update the config file.
Chronology for Events Incident Reports

When describing events, use chronological order. Timelines should flow forward. Jumping around confuses readers trying to reconstruct what happened.

Events have causation. A → B → C. Scrambling the order obscures cause and effect.

Before We fixed it at 3pm. The alert fired at 2pm. Users reported issues at 2:15pm.
After 2:00pm - Alert fired. 2:15pm - Users reported issues. 3:00pm - Fix deployed.
Problem Before Solution Proposals

Establish the problem before presenting solutions. Readers won't care about your solution until they understand (and feel) the problem.

Solutions without problems feel like answers to questions nobody asked.

Before We should migrate to Kubernetes. Here's the implementation plan...
After Our current deployment takes 4 hours and fails 30% of the time. Kubernetes would reduce this to 10 minutes with 99% success.
Action Before Rationale Runbooks

In emergency procedures, tell people what to do first, then explain why. During an incident, engineers need action steps immediately.

At 3am with the site down, nobody wants a history lesson before the fix.

Before The cache can overflow when traffic exceeds 10K QPS because the TTL is set too high, so you should restart the cache service.
After Restart the cache service: `sudo systemctl restart cache`. (This clears overflow caused by high TTL during traffic spikes.)

Document Architecture

Different situations call for different document structures.

Incident Report After something broke

Document what happened, why, and how to prevent recurrence. The goal is learning, not blame.

Structure: Executive Summary → Impact → Timeline → Root Cause → Remediation → Prevention

Use Post-mortems, outage reports, security incident documentation
Technical Proposal Need approval/resources

Pitch an idea to stakeholders. Convince them the problem is real and your solution is worth the investment.

Structure: Problem Statement → Proposed Solution → Cost/Benefit Analysis → Implementation Plan → Risks

Use New tool adoption, infrastructure changes, process improvements
Decision Memo Leadership choosing options

Present options fairly for decision-makers. Include clear criteria and your recommendation (if asked).

Structure: Recommendation → Options → Analysis Against Criteria → Trade-offs → Next Steps

Use Vendor selection, architecture decisions, build vs. buy
Design Document Before building complexity

Blueprint for implementation. Capture requirements, architecture, and decisions before writing code.

Structure: Requirements → Architecture Overview → Alternatives Considered → Detailed Design → Testing Strategy

Use New services, major refactors, API design, system integration
Operational Runbook On-call procedures

Step-by-step guide for operators. Optimize for 3am use: action first, explanation second.

Structure: Quick Reference → Procedures → Troubleshooting → Escalation → Contacts

Use Alert response, deployment procedures, disaster recovery
Status Update Recurring communication

Keep stakeholders informed. Be predictable, scannable, and honest about risks.

Structure: Summary/Health → Progress Since Last Update → Blockers → Next Steps → Timeline Status

Use Weekly reports, project updates, migration progress
